# Chapter 002 Draft

Mara read the first checkpoint receipt twice before the second bell.

The line that mattered was not hidden: token entry barred for next checkpoint. She had not missed it. She had hoped it would mean the token itself, not every door attached to it.

At the second checkpoint, the clerk placed three witness strips on the table. One white, one gray, one blue. The rules board said: choose a strip before the bell, no exchanges after mark.

Lio stood at her left shoulder. He had come because his name was already attached to hers. He had not come because he forgave her.

Renn leaned against the rail with a blue strip already tucked under two fingers. "Gray gets called last," she said. "White is clean."

Mara looked at the board. The notch from the first checkpoint had been beside the left queue. Here the same notch sat beside the white strip.

If Renn was lying, white was a trap. If Renn was helping, refusing the hint would waste the only minute Mara had. She could ask Lio to verify, but then he would know she had taken a competitor's word before his.

The bell rope twitched.

## Evidence: token-block

Mara pushed the pierced token across the table anyway. The clerk did not touch it.

"Barred," he said. "Witness path only."

The word carried down the rail. Renn's smile thinned. Lio looked at the token, then at Mara.

She swallowed the easy answer. "I need the witness order," she said to Lio. "Not the whole rule. Just whether white is clean."

"Who told you white mattered?"

Mara did not answer fast enough.

## Evidence: debt-worsens

Lio checked the clerk's side slate, then chose gray, not white. He signed before Mara could ask why.

"Gray is delayed," he said. "Not last. White is clean only if the witness is unmarked."

His sleeve was still covering the red audit mark. Both of them knew what that meant.

The clerk accepted the gray strip and stamped Mara's receipt with a delay mark. It was not a failure. It was also not a pass.

Renn stopped smiling.

Mara followed Lio out of the line. The gray strip had bought her entry, but it had also told Lio enough. She had used Renn's hint, hidden the source, and asked him to carry the risk of correcting it.

"I thought I was saving your name," Mara said.

"No," Lio said. "You were saving your choice."

The second checkpoint remained open, delayed by one mark. The question was no longer only which signal was reliable. It was whether Mara could use help without turning it into another debt.
